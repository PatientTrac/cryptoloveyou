Exchanges charge fees in three main areas:

  • Trading fees — a percentage per buy/sell order, split into maker (adds liquidity) and taker (removes liquidity) rates
  • Deposit/withdrawal fees — vary by payment method; bank transfers are usually free, card deposits cost more
  • Network (gas) fees — blockchain transaction costs passed directly to users

Holding a platform's native token (e.g. BNB on Binance, BIT on Bybit) typically unlocks 25–50% fee discounts. Always compare the full round-trip cost, not just the headline maker rate.

A centralised exchange (CEX) is operated by a company that holds your funds and matches orders through an internal order book — similar to a traditional brokerage. CEXs offer superior liquidity, fast execution, fiat on-ramps, and customer support.

A decentralised exchange (DEX) runs on blockchain smart contracts, enabling peer-to-peer trades without an intermediary holding your assets. DEXs are non-custodial and grant access to tokens not yet listed on major platforms, but typically carry higher gas costs, lower liquidity, and no consumer protection or fiat deposits.

Crypto regulation evolves rapidly. As of 2026, most major economies — including the US, UK, EU member states, Canada, Australia, Singapore, and Japan — permit retail crypto trading, provided exchanges hold the required licences and comply with AML/KYC rules. A small number of countries still restrict or prohibit crypto activity outright.

We strongly recommend verifying the regulatory landscape in your specific jurisdiction before depositing funds, and always using an exchange that is fully licensed in your region. This content does not constitute legal advice.
